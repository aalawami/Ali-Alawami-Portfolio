COMP SCI 200 - 400 Programming I, II, & III
The Programming Fundamentals Sequence is a comprehensive series of three courses that cover computer science fundamentals, object-oriented programming, and advanced programming concepts. Starting with Programming I, students learn basics such as problem abstraction, variable usage, and flow control. In Programming II, the focus shifts to object-oriented programming and data structures like lists, stacks, and queues. The sequence culminates with Programming III, where students dive into advanced topics such as balanced search trees, graph traversal algorithms, and lambda functions. Throughout the series, students develop practical skills in solving real-world problems, using good programming practices and the latest language features, tools, and conventions.

COMP SCI 570 Intro to Human-Computer Interaction
The course focuses on user-centered software design, covering principles and methods for understanding user needs, designing and prototyping interface solutions, and evaluating their usability. The course also includes month-long group projects to apply these principles and methods in designing web-based, mobile, and embodied interfaces.

COMP SCI 540 Intro to Artificial Intelligence
The course covers various topics such as knowledge-based search techniques, automatic deduction, knowledge representation using predicate logic, machine learning, and probabilistic reasoning. The course also explores how these principles can be applied to different tasks such as problem-solving, data mining, game-playing, natural language understanding, computer vision, speech recognition, and robotics.

COMP SCI 537 Intro to Operating Systems
The course covers topics related to computer hardware, including input-output hardware, interrupt handling, and properties of magnetic tapes, discs, and drums. The course also covers associative memories and virtual address translation techniques. Additionally, the course explores different types of computer systems, such as batch processing, time sharing, and real-time systems, and related concepts such as scheduling, resource allocation, modular software systems, performance measurement, and system evaluation.
▼ 		Example
COMP SCI 559 Computer Graphics
The course provides an overview of computer graphics, covering topics such as image representation, formation, presentation, composition, and manipulation. The course also includes modeling, transformation, and display of geometric objects in both two and three dimensions, representation of curves and surfaces, rendering, animation, multimedia, and visualization.
▼ 		Example
COMP SCI 564 Database Management Systems
The course covers the fundamentals of database management systems, including an explanation of what a database management system is and the different data models used to structure the logical view of the database, such as relational, hierarchical, and network models. Students will gain hands-on experience with relational and network-based database systems and will learn implementation techniques for database systems, including file organization, query processing, concurrency control, rollback and recovery, integrity and consistency, and view implementation.
▼ 		Example
COMP SCI 240 Intro to Discrete Mathematics
The course covers the basic concepts of logic, sets, partial order, and other relations, as well as functions. The course also covers the basic concepts of mathematics, including definitions, proofs, sets, functions, and relations, with a focus on discrete structures such as integers, bits, strings, trees, and graphs. Additionally, the course includes topics such as propositional logic, Boolean algebra, and predicate logic, as well as mathematical induction and recursion. Students will also learn about invariants and algorithmic correctness, recurrences and asymptotic growth analysis, and the fundamentals of counting.

COMP SCI 252 Intro to Computer Engineering
The course covers the design of logic components built with transistors, rudimentary Boolean algebra, basic combinational logic design, basic synchronous sequential logic design, basic computer organization and design, and introductory machine- and assembly-language programming.

COMP SCI 354 Machine Organization and Programming
The course provides an introduction to the fundamental structures of computer systems and the C programming language, with a focus on their low-level interrelationships and impacts on performance. Topics covered in the course include the virtual address space and virtual memory, the heap and dynamic memory management, the memory hierarchy and caching, assembly language and the stack, communication and interrupts/signals, as well as compiling and assemblers/linkers.

CURRIC 277 Video Games and Learning

CURRIC 357, 432 Game Design I, II
These two courses, CURRIC 357 and 432, are focused on game design. Game Design I introduces students to the fundamentals of game design and interactive system design. The course is useful for those pursuing careers in educational game design or design fields. Game Design II focuses on the cultural and social impact of games and allows students to create a capstone project of an analog or digital game. The course emphasizes iterative design practices and incorporates user feedback. Throughout both courses, students explore various types of independent games and the platforms used to create them.

The course is an advanced level course focused on educational game development. It covers advanced game development content and its application in educational games. Students will study specific advanced content such as adaptive agents and real-time multiplayer, as well as gain a broader understanding of the underlying principles across different content domains.

ART 107, & 428 Intro to Digital Forms,
& Digital Imaging Studio
ART 107 and 428 are courses that focus on digital media techniques and practices in digital fine arts focusing on the tools Adobe Photoshop, Adobe Premiere Pro, Adobe After Effects. ART 107 provides an introduction to various digital media techniques for artists and designers, such as digital imaging and 3D digital modeling, while emphasizing creative development and technical skill-building. ART 428 explores current and historical practices in the digital fine arts, allowing students to create and manipulate static and moving imagery, with implications for digital drawing, painting, print-making, video editing/post-production, and 2D animation. Both courses equip students with a broad skill set and understanding of digital media applicable in various artistic contexts.

MATH 221, 222, 234 Calculus I, II, III
In Calculus I (MATH 221), students are introduced to differential and integral calculus as well as plane analytic geometry. The course covers applications of calculus and transcendental functions. Calculus II (MATH 222) focuses on techniques of integration, including improper integrals, as well as first order ordinary differential equations, sequences and series, Taylor series, and vector geometry in two and three dimensions. In Calculus III (MATH 234), students are introduced to the calculus of functions of several variables, including calculus on parameterized curves, derivatives of functions of several variables, multiple integrals, and vector calculus. Together, these courses provide a comprehensive introduction to calculus and its applications in a variety of fields.

Additionally, the course includes the study of symmetric matrices.

CLASSICS 370 Classical Mythology
The course will introduce students to the major characters and stories of Greek mythology. It includes reading ancient texts in translation, such as Homer's Odyssey and plays by Sophocles and Euripides. The course will enable students to interpret myths in various forms and contexts and explore how the Greeks used myths to address personal, political, and social issues. Additionally, the course will highlight the significance of reading these ancient stories in the modern world.

ENGL 155 Myths of Modernity
The course delves into the impact of the seventeenth century scientific revolution in Europe, which elevated reason as the most important mental faculty and its effects on society. The course questions the notion of modernity by analyzing its core beliefs and the mythic language surrounding them. Through literary analysis of texts, the course examines how modernity is perceived and how it persists in contemporary society.
